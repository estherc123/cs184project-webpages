Overview

For the first part of the project, we are interested in creating reflective, refractive, and glass surfaces. This is done by calculating the BRDF of each of the surfaces. The reflective and the refraction does not have a lambertian fallout for its illuminance. For the glass surfaces it is a mixture of some reflective and mostly refractive material that are proportionally weighted based on the Schlickâ€™s reflection coefficient. In the second part of the project, we implemented microfacet models of isotropic rough conductor surfaces that only reflect. We first need to compute the BRDF of the metal surface, which is dependent on the half vector h between view vector and incident light. Then we assume the surface normal is approximately normally distributed, and the distribution is dependent on the roughness of surface. Using importance sampling for incoming light direction, along with Fresnelâ€™s term and the shadow-masking term, we could then compute the BRDF, and thus render a pretty metallic surface.

Part 1

Implementation

In this section we have implemented the reflection function, in which essentially light incoming at a solid angle towards a surface will bounce at an angle symmetrical with respect to the surface normal in object space. Then we have implemented BRDF of glass material to have a deterministic trajectory given a sampled input solid angle and that there is no lambertian tradeoff so we multiply by absolute cos(theta). Then we implemented refraction which will check whether or not the light is entering at a solid angle or exiting the solid angle and determine the wi solid angle direction. Using the refraction function we can implement BRDF of refractive materials by accounting for no lambertian tradeoff and accounting for radiance concentration/dispersion behavior. Then for glass materials, it is a combination of reflection and refraction. First check whether it has total internal reflection so it will act as a reflective surface. Otherwise we use an approximation of the Schlickâ€™s reflection coefficient (R) to give a coin-flip probability of ray reflecting or refracting with the pdf being R and 1 - R respectively.
